Entry 031 · Wave 01 · The Foundational Canon

The Creature That Became Anything

Alpha’s simplest copy spell turned every creature on the battlefield into a blue card—and then spent nearly a decade proving that a simple fantasy could be one of Magic’s hardest rules problems.

I · The blank

The creature with no body

Most creature cards ask whether their body is worth their cost. Clone asked a stranger question: whose body would you like it to have?

Printed in Limited Edition Alpha as a four-mana 0/0, Clone did not arrive with a stable size, color story, combat role, or even a useful existence of its own. It was a blank permanent waiting for the battlefield to supply its meaning. A Serra Angel across the table could become your Serra Angel. A Shivan Dragon could become your Dragon. A utility creature could be duplicated because its text mattered more than its statistics.

The card’s promise was compact enough to explain in one sentence and deep enough to occupy rules managers for years. That contrast is the heart of Clone’s history. It is one of the cleanest ideas Richard Garfield placed in Alpha—and one of the hardest for the early game engine to express consistently.

You may have this creature enter as a copy of any creature on the battlefield.

II · The first mirror

Alpha’s blank space

Alpha was full of cards that established categories: Counterspell named permission, Control Magic named theft, and Clone named the creature-copy effect so completely that later designs are still called “Clones” even when the word never appears on them.

The original card’s 0/0 frame mattered. It communicated that there was no creature underneath the effect. Clone was not a weak Shapeshifter that received a temporary bonus. Its characteristics were replaced as it entered. The physical card on the table remained Clone, but within the game it became the copied creature’s name, mana cost, color, types, rules text, power, and toughness.

That was a startling proposition in 1993. Magic cards appeared to be fixed objects. Clone suggested that the rules could treat the printed face as only a starting instruction.

Clone made every creature in play part of blue’s card pool.The opponent supplied the threat. Blue supplied the second copy.
III · The sibling

The simpler shapeshifter

Alpha contained two famous creature copies. Vesuvan Doppelganger cost one additional mana, stayed blue, and could select a new creature during each upkeep. Clone was cheaper and permanent: choose once as it entered, then live with the result.

The distinction gave the cards different emotional textures. The Doppelganger was a mutable character, always threatening to become something else. Clone was a spell disguised as a creature. It converted four mana into the best available body and then stopped asking questions.

That simplicity made Clone the enduring template. When players say a new card is “a Clone,” they usually mean an enters-as-a-copy creature, not a permanent that repeatedly transforms. Vesuvan Doppelganger was the more spectacular card. Clone became the grammar.

IV · The color

Blue borrows identity

Copying became blue because it expressed knowledge, illusion, adaptation, and appropriation rather than biological growth. Green could make the largest creature. Black could return one from the graveyard. White could answer it cleanly. Blue could study it and manufacture an equivalent.

Clone’s balance depended on the battlefield. It could never be the first great creature in play. It demanded that someone else establish the ceiling. That dependence made the effect feel interactive even when the result was powerful: the card’s value rose and fell with the creatures already available.

It also captured a durable blue fantasy. The color did not need to own the strongest monster in its deck. It only needed access to the idea of that monster.

Julie Baroh’s original CloneThe Alpha image treats imitation as an uncanny doubling of the human form rather than a conventional fantasy monster. The repeated figures became one of the game’s earliest visual statements about unstable identity.
V · The machinery

What Clone actually copies

Modern Oracle wording makes the play pattern look effortless, but copy effects operate through a precise set of distinctions. Clone does not target. Its choice is made as it enters, so a creature with hexproof, shroud, or protection can still be copied. There is no moment when an ordinary 0/0 Clone sits on the battlefield before becoming something else.

It copies

The chosen creature’s copiable name, mana cost, color, types, rules text, power, toughness, and other printed or copy-defined characteristics.

It does not copy

Tapped status, damage, counters, attached Auras or Equipment, or ordinary effects that have modified the creature.

Enters abilities work

Because Clone enters as the creature, any relevant enters abilities trigger and “as this enters” choices are made.

Copies of copies continue

If the chosen creature is already copying something, Clone receives the characteristics established by that copy effect.

X becomes zero

If the copied card has X in its mana cost, the copied object treats X as zero outside the stack.

You may decline

If no creature is chosen, Clone enters as its printed 0/0 self and is usually put into the graveyard by state-based actions.

These details turned a single sentence into a foundation for a large portion of the Comprehensive Rules. Copy effects are intuitive at the table only because decades of rules work now support them.

VI · The champion

A copy in the first world champion

Clone’s most important early tournament appearance came as a singleton in Zak Dolan’s 1994 World Championship deck. The list was a remarkable control toolbox containing Serra Angels, Stasis, artifact prison pieces, removal, the Power Nine, and both of Alpha’s creature-copy cards.

In that environment, a singleton Clone was not expected to define every game. It increased the deck’s flexibility. It could become another Serra Angel when Dolan needed a clock, duplicate an opposing utility creature, or turn the best threat on either side into an additional resource.

The presence of both Clone and Vesuvan Doppelganger in Magic’s first world champion is a reminder that early control decks did not always separate “answer” from “finisher.” A copy creature could be either, depending entirely on what the game had produced.

VII · The missing years

The card that stopped working

Clone moved through Alpha, Beta, Unlimited, and Revised, then disappeared from ordinary tournament-legal sets. The absence was not because the concept had become unpopular. Wizards designers have repeatedly described the opposite: the card was beloved, but the rules could not reliably support it.

Early Magic had to answer questions that now seem routine. What happens when Clone copies another Clone? What characteristics survive a change of zone? Does it copy a creature altered by an enchantment? When exactly is the choice made? What happens to enters abilities? Each answer had consequences for other cards.

Mark Rosewater later summarized the problem bluntly: for years, Clone existed as a known design that the game could not comfortably print. The effect’s disappearance became an unusual part of its identity. It was an Alpha original waiting for the rules system to catch up with the fantasy.

VIII · The substitute

The Clone that became Morphling

R&D attempted to bring Clone back in Urza’s Saga. Art had already been commissioned as a visual echo of the Alpha original, but the proposed rules solution failed late in development. The card had to become something else.

The famous replacement

Morphling

The abandoned Clone slot was redesigned into Morphling, the blue finisher later nicknamed “Superman.” Its art still carries the visual DNA of the card it was supposed to reprint.

This accident is one of Magic’s most productive design failures. A rules problem prevented an old favorite from returning and created one of the defining creatures of late-1990s tournament Magic instead. Clone’s absence cast a shadow large enough to produce a classic.

IX · The return

The return in Onslaught

By 2002, copy rules had been cleaned up enough for Clone to return in Onslaught. The set’s tribal themes made the reprint especially appropriate: creatures and creature types mattered everywhere, and the simplest creature copy could participate in any tribe represented on the battlefield.

The Onslaught version raised Clone from uncommon to rare and gave it new art by Carl Critchlow. More importantly, it marked the end of the card’s exile. From that point forward, Clone could once again appear in normal sets without the game collapsing under the questions it created.

Its return also changed expectations. Copy creatures were no longer a dangerous corner of design. They became a reusable tool, and R&D began exploring versions that were cheaper, restricted, instant-speed, temporary, legendary-friendly, or capable of copying other permanent types.

X · The weapon

The accidental legend killer

For a long stretch of Magic history, Clone carried a tactical function that its modern text does not advertise: it could answer legendary creatures without targeting them. Under the 2004–2013 legend rule, two legendary permanents with the same name were both put into their owners’ graveyards. Clone could enter as an opposing legend and cause the pair to disappear.

That made the card unusually attractive in sideboards when a format revolved around a difficult legendary threat. Hexproof or protection did not matter because Clone’s choice did not target. The copy effect became a strange blue removal spell.

The 2013 legend-rule change ended that exact exchange. Each player could control a permanent with the same legendary name, and a player controlling multiples chose one to keep. Clone remained useful, but it no longer automatically assassinated the opponent’s legend merely by matching it.

XI · The rate

Where four mana was enough

Historic

Early constructed

A flexible singleton in control decks, capable of becoming either the opponent’s best threat or another copy of a protected finisher.

Returning role

Onslaught and core sets

A recognizable rare whose value depended on creature quality, Limited boards, and occasional legend-rule utility.

Enduring home

Commander and casual play

Multiplayer tables naturally supply expensive creatures and enters abilities, turning a generic copy into a constantly changing story.

Clone was never guaranteed to be efficient. Four mana for the best creature already on the battlefield is excellent only when the battlefield cooperates. In low-curve formats, the card can be too slow or copy something worth less than four mana. Against creature-light control, it may have no body at all.

Its best environments are rich in high-impact creatures and enters abilities. Multiplayer Commander magnifies both conditions. Three opponents provide more targets, and modern creature design ensures that copying a creature often means copying an immediate effect as well as a body.

XII · The descendants

An entire family of mirrors

Clone became the baseline from which later copy creatures advertise their difference. Phantasmal Image is cheaper but fragile. Stunt Double has flash. Spark Double can copy planeswalkers and evade the legend rule. Clever Impersonator reaches nearly every nonland permanent. Phyrexian Metamorph crosses the boundary between creature and artifact.

The expanded template

Phyrexian Metamorph

By New Phyrexia, “a Clone” could cost three mana and life, copy an artifact, and exploit both creature and artifact synergies. The original template had become a design platform.

Other descendants alter power and toughness, add creature types, create tokens, copy only friendly creatures, copy from graveyards, or repeatedly change identities. Yet the original question survives in every version: what is the most useful thing this object could be right now?

XIII · The image

Three visions of imitation

Julie Baroh’s Alpha art presents duplication as eerie bodily repetition. The figures are recognizable as copies, but their arrangement feels ritualistic and unnatural. It is an image about identity before it is an image about combat.

Carl Critchlow’s Onslaught version shifted toward a more overt fantasy transformation, fitting a set filled with creature types and morphing bodies. Kev Walker’s core-set art made the concept more cinematic and immediately readable for newer players.

The changing art tracks the card’s changing role. In Alpha, Clone was a conceptual experiment. In Onslaught, it was a recovered rules technology. In the core sets, it became a standard part of Magic literacy.

XIV · The table

The rare that asked a question

“What are you copying?” was never a rules formality. It was the entire personality of the card.

For new players, Clone produced a small revelation: the best card in your deck did not have to be printed in your deck. The battlefield itself could supply it.

For experienced players, the question became technical. Which creature offers the best enters trigger? Which copy survives removal? Is it better to duplicate your own engine or match an opponent’s finisher?

Compulsive Research · Player-memory synthesis

Clone also created stories that a fixed creature could not. One game it was a Dragon. The next it was a mana creature copied out of desperation. Sometimes it copied a legendary threat as removal. Sometimes there was nothing worth becoming and the card waited uselessly in hand.

XV · The verdict

What remains

Clone belongs in the foundational canon because it defined one of Magic’s most durable mechanical nouns. The game now contains hundreds of cards that copy creatures, permanents, spells, abilities, and objects in other zones, but players still describe many of them by reference to this four-mana Alpha uncommon.

Its competitive history is respectable rather than dominant: a place in the first World Championship deck, recurring core-set appearances, tactical legend-rule use, and continuing Old School and casual relevance. Its rules and design history is much larger. Clone exposed the difference between a card’s printed face and its game characteristics, disappeared because the rules could not support it, indirectly created Morphling, and returned when the system matured.

The card’s final achievement is linguistic. “Clone” no longer means only one card. It means an entire category of Magic design.

XVII · The record

Printings and sources

Clone has one of the clearest visual timelines among Alpha creatures. Julie Baroh’s original moved through the first core sets, Carl Critchlow marked the card’s rules-enabled return in Onslaught, and Kev Walker’s interpretation carried it through the modern core-set era. The 30th Anniversary printing returned to the original visual identity in a non-tournament-legal commemorative product.
