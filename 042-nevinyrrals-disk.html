The Table Had One Turn Left

Nevinyrral’s Disk gave every color a visible apocalypse, clearing artifacts, creatures, and enchantments while forcing the entire table to play beneath the threat of the next untap step.

I · The Warning

The Table Had One Turn Left

Nevinyrral’s Disk does not surprise the battlefield. It arrives tapped, sits in plain sight, and gives every player one full turn to decide what matters enough to save, spend, sacrifice, counter, or destroy before the world ends.

That warning is the card’s identity. A conventional sweeper clears what has already happened. The Disk changes what happens next. Creatures remain in hand. Enchantments are withheld. Artifacts are activated early. Opponents attack the player who controls the button because everyone understands what untapping will mean.

For early black and blue decks, the artifact was more than a board wipe. It was access to kinds of removal their colors did not naturally possess. It destroyed the enchantments black could not touch, the creatures blue could not efficiently kill, and the artifacts threatening either strategy. Four generic mana bought an apocalypse almost anyone could cast.

II · The Device

Alpha’s colorless apocalypse

Alpha distributed mass destruction unevenly. White received Wrath of God. Red received Earthquake. Black had Pestilence and sacrifice, but no clean answer to every creature, artifact, and enchantment. Blue could counter threats before they resolved but had difficulty permanently removing those already on the battlefield.

Nevinyrral’s Disk gave every color a shared emergency exit. Its generic mana cost made it splashable in the literal sense that no splash was required. A mono-black deck, mono-blue deck, five-color control deck, or artifact prison deck could all reserve four slots for the same promise: if the battlefield became unmanageable, reset it.

The original card was a Mono Artifact, an early type used for artifacts whose abilities required tapping. The modern card retains the physical rhythm without the obsolete type line: cast it, wait, untap it, and decide whether the table still deserves to exist.

The Disk’s first effect was not destruction. It was making every permanent temporary.A visible apocalypse changes play before it happens
III · The Delay

Why it enters tapped

If Nevinyrral’s Disk entered untapped, five mana would buy an immediate colorless sweeper that destroyed three permanent types. Entering tapped creates counterplay and transforms the card from a spell into a looming event.

The opponent receives a window to attack, remove the Disk, counter the activation with a specialized effect, phase out important permanents, or force the controller to detonate earlier than intended. The controller must survive the turn in which four mana produced no immediate battlefield change.

That delay also creates bluffing. Once the Disk untaps, its controller may never need to activate it. The table must behave as though the activation is available, and that behavioral restraint can be more valuable than the cards eventually destroyed.

IV · The Boundary

Everything except the lands

The Disk destroys artifacts, creatures, and enchantments. Lands survive unless they also possess one of those types. Planeswalkers are not named and ordinarily survive as well.

Destroyed

The developed board

Creatures, artifact mana, Equipment, prison pieces, Auras, global enchantments, and artifact creatures all disappear together.

Preserved

The means to rebuild

Ordinary lands remain, allowing both players to redeploy immediately after the reset.

Conditional

Additional card types

An artifact land or animated creature-land is destroyed because it matches a named type; an ordinary planeswalker is not.

Leaving lands intact separates the Disk from Armageddon and Obliterate. It does not restart the entire game. It clears the investments made with mana while preserving the mana itself, turning the next hand and draw step into the beginning of a new battlefield.

V · The Activation

What Nevinyrral’s Disk actually does

The Disk enters the battlefield tapped. Its activated ability costs one mana and tapping the Disk. When the ability resolves, every artifact, creature, and enchantment is destroyed simultaneously.

The Disk is not sacrificed as part of the activation cost. It remains on the battlefield while the ability is on the stack and is normally destroyed by its own effect when that effect resolves. Removing the Disk after activation does not stop the ability. Once activated, the destruction exists independently of its source.

Because everything is destroyed simultaneously, leaves-the-battlefield and death triggers see the complete event. A creature with indestructible survives. A permanent that regenerates survives after becoming tapped, having its damage removed, and being removed from combat under the applicable regeneration rules.

The important distinction. Destroying the Disk in response to its activation does not save the battlefield. The activation must be countered, its targets do not exist, or the affected permanents must be protected from destruction.
Mark Tedin’s original Nevinyrral’s DiskThe artifact appears less like a weapon than a sealed mechanism whose geometry contains a catastrophe. Its small scale makes the implied radius of destruction more unsettling.
VI · The Leverage

The threat was often stronger than the blast

An untapped Disk turns every new permanent into a wager. The opponent may deploy enough pressure to force activation, but each additional card increases the loss when the Disk detonates. Holding back preserves resources but gives the controller time.

This is the classic tension of a visible sweeper. The controller is not merely trading one card for several. The controller is controlling the rate at which the opponent is willing to commit cards to the battlefield.

A skilled Disk player could wait until the final safe moment, absorbing damage or tolerating inconvenient permanents to increase the eventual exchange. A skilled opponent could present just enough pressure to demand the explosion without losing the entire hand.

VII · The Access

The answer colors were not supposed to have

Black historically kills creatures and attacks hands, but it has difficulty destroying artifacts and enchantments. Blue counters spells and returns permanents, but permanent mass removal is not central to its identity. Nevinyrral’s Disk allowed both colors to bypass those limitations.

The cost was speed and self-destruction. The artifact announced itself, entered tapped, and erased the controller’s own permanents. That price made the effect acceptable as a colorless tool rather than a routine spell available without compromise.

For mono-colored control, this mattered enormously. A resolved Circle of Protection: Black, Moat, The Abyss, creature swarm, or opposing artifact engine no longer represented a permanent failure of deck construction. The Disk could eventually answer it.

VIII · The Bargain

Necropotence and rebuilding first

Nevinyrral’s Disk destroys enchantments, including the controller’s own Necropotence. That apparent conflict became part of the strategy rather than a reason to exclude the artifact.

Necropotence allowed black decks to exchange life for a stocked hand. Once the necessary cards had been acquired, the Disk could clear creatures, artifacts, hate enchantments, and Necropotence itself. Both players lost the battlefield, but the Necro player often began the next game with more cards.

The Disk also answered permanent types black otherwise struggled to remove. Losing Necropotence was acceptable when the alternative was losing the game to something the deck could not meaningfully touch.

IX · The Namesake

Nevinyrral’s Necro

At the 1996 World Championship, Scott Johns reached the quarterfinals with a mono-black deck recorded as “Nevinyrral’s Necro.” The name placed the Disk beside Necropotence as a defining engine rather than a generic utility card.

Johns played three copies in a documented 61-card main deck and another in the sideboard. Icequake and Strip Mine attacked mana. Hymn to Tourach and Hypnotic Specter attacked the hand. The Disk ensured that whatever reached the battlefield could still be erased.

Henry Stern and Mark Justice also placed Necropotence decks in the same Top 8 with four and three main-deck Disks respectively. The artifact was not an eccentric choice. It was part of how Black Summer’s dominant strategy protected itself from the permanent-based answers opponents brought to defeat it.

X · The Permission

Draw-Go’s battlefield reset

Randy Buehler’s 1998 World Championship deck represented the opposite color and a similar need. Mono-blue Draw-Go wanted to counter everything important, spend unused mana on Whispers of the Muse, and win eventually with Rainbow Efreet or animated lands.

No permission deck counters every spell. A cheap threat may resolve before countermagic is ready. Several threats may overwhelm the available answers. An uncounterable or tactically protected permanent may enter play. Four Nevinyrral’s Disks gave Draw-Go a way to repair those failures.

The artifact’s delay suited the deck. Counters protected it during the vulnerable turn, and the threat of activation discouraged further development. Once the Disk cleared the board, the blue deck returned to the phase of the game it preferred: both players with mana, but only one player with a hand full of permission.

XI · The Escape

Activate, then save the Disk

Because the Disk is not sacrificed to activate, its controller can respond to the activation by removing it from the battlefield. A bounce spell can return it to hand. A sacrifice outlet can convert it into another resource. The destruction still resolves.

This creates a classic asymmetry: activate the Disk, cast Boomerang or Capsize on it, let the board disappear, and recast the saved artifact later. The same principle works with blink or phase effects that remove the Disk from the battlefield before resolution.

The sequence is mana-intensive, but in slow control mirrors it changes a one-use emergency button into a recurring plan. The controller preserves the very card responsible for destroying everything else.

XII · The Recurrence

Indestructible, regeneration, and repetition

If Nevinyrral’s Disk is indestructible, its own ability does not destroy it. The artifact remains tapped on the battlefield and can untap for another activation. Darksteel Forge creates the most famous version, making the controller’s artifacts—including the Disk—indestructible while the Disk destroys opposing creatures and enchantments.

Older cards capable of regenerating artifacts could also preserve it because the effect destroys rather than sacrifices. Modern Commander decks use indestructibility, recursion, untapping, and artifact retrieval to threaten repeated resets.

This is possible because the original design did not include a sacrifice cost. One missing word created decades of interactions in which the apocalypse survived itself.

XIII · The Reversal

Larry Niven written backward

“Nevinyrral” is “Larry Niven” reversed. The name points toward Niven’s The Magic Goes Away stories, in which magic consumes a finite resource called mana and a spinning device demonstrates how that resource can be exhausted from an area.

Magic borrowed more than a hidden name. The concept of mana as a resource tied to place and the image of a disk that annihilates magical potential resonate directly with the card’s function. Artifacts, creatures, and enchantments—the visible products of magic—are removed while the lands that produced the mana remain.

For decades, the card existed before the character. Players knew Nevinyrral only through the possessive apostrophe and the machine carrying his name.

XIV · The Lich

The lich finally receives a face

Later Dominaria lore identified Nevinyrral as a lich and necromancer of Urborg who destroyed his own city rather than allow enemies to claim its magical knowledge. The Disk became his final act of spite: annihilation as denial.

Commander Legends finally printed Nevinyrral, Urborg Tyrant in 2020. The legendary creature protects itself from artifacts, creatures, and enchantments, creates Zombies when it enters based on recent deaths, and can reproduce the Disk’s destruction when it dies.

The design closed a circle almost twenty-eight years old. A card named after an unseen owner became the mechanical blueprint for the owner’s eventual card.

XV · The Tempo

Why the warning became too slow

Competitive Magic grew faster and more immediate. Creatures began generating value as they entered. Planeswalkers created resources while surviving the Disk. Artifact and enchantment removal became more available. Four mana spent on a tapped permanent could concede too much tempo.

Modern sweepers often resolve immediately, exile rather than destroy, or leave behind a body and additional value. Control decks became less willing to reveal the answer a full turn before using it.

The Disk did not become weak. The price of waiting became larger. Its modern competitive appearances tend to involve formats, decks, or matchups where universal coverage and artifact synergy matter more than immediate speed.

XVI · The Table

The Commander institution

Commander restored the Disk’s natural scale. Multiplayer boards accumulate creatures, artifacts, enchantments, tokens, Equipment, and engines from several players. One card capable of destroying nearly all of them can generate enormous card advantage.

The visible warning becomes political. Casting the Disk announces that the controller can reset the table, but also invites opponents to negotiate, redirect attacks, remove the artifact, or force its use. The controller may receive protection from players who fear another opponent’s battlefield more.

Its survival interactions also become practical. Avacyn, Angel of Hope, Darksteel Forge, untap effects, artifact recursion, and Nevinyrral himself turn the Disk from a panic button into a deck-building axis.

XVII · The Bloodline

The descendants of the Disk

Oblivion Stone replaces the waiting period with a larger activation cost and fate counters that can preserve chosen permanents. Perilous Vault exiles rather than destroys and removes itself as part of the cost. Boompile is cheaper to activate but submits the apocalypse to a coin flip.

The configurable descendant

Oblivion Stone

Oblivion Stone preserves the Disk’s colorless reset while allowing its controller to mark selected permanents with fate counters. It costs more to detonate but can make the destruction deliberately one-sided.

All Is Dust makes each player sacrifice colored permanents. Ugin, the Spirit Dragon attaches a selective reset to a planeswalker. The Stone Brain shares none of the effect but preserves the tradition of artifacts whose names promise something terrible once activated.

The descendants alter speed, scope, survival, and protection, but the central question remains Nevinyrral’s: how much warning should a colorless card give before it removes the battlefield?

XVIII · The Image

Two visions of annihilation

Mark Tedin’s original art depicts a compact disk surrounded by darkness and controlled energy. Its scale is ambiguous. The device may fit in a hand, sit within a larger machine, or represent an impossible mechanism viewed without environmental context.

Steve Argyle’s From the Vault interpretation expands the artifact into an ornate black-metal device with blue power burning through its rings. The newer image makes the Disk feel legendary and catastrophic, an object worthy of the lore later built around its owner.

The two artworks approach the same fear differently. Tedin presents a mysterious machine whose purpose is unclear. Argyle presents a weapon whose purpose is unmistakable.

XIX · The Habitats

A life across formats

Historic Standard

A control cornerstone

Necropotence and Draw-Go used the Disk as a universal answer through some of the most important tournaments of the 1990s.

Modern and Eternal

Legal, highly specialized

Modern Horizons 2 made it Modern legal, while Legacy and Vintage have always valued the effect more historically than currently.

Commander

A multiplayer staple

Large boards, artifact recursion, indestructibility, and politics restore the scale at which a visible universal reset is most meaningful.

The Disk is not Pauper legal because it has no qualifying common printing. In Cube, it is strongest in environments that support artifact control, slower games, and colors that need access to a broad sweeper.

XX · The Feeling

What players remember

The Disk was tapped. Everyone knew exactly how long they had.
Nevinyrral’s Disk created suspense by placing the answer on the battlefield before permitting the answer to function.

Players remember counting whether the Disk’s controller could survive one more attack. They remember holding creatures in hand, destroying their own value artifacts before the blast, and deciding whether forcing the activation was worth losing the current board.

They remember the old Mark Tedin machinery, the apostrophe in the impossible name, and the revelation that the name was Larry Niven backward. Most of all, they remember the untap step when a permanent stopped being an artifact and became a threat to everything.

XXII · The Object

Printings and sources

Nevinyrral’s Disk carried Mark Tedin’s original artwork from Alpha through Fifth Edition. After more than a decade without a new paper treatment, From the Vault: Relics introduced Steve Argyle’s interpretation in 2010. That artwork became the modern standard across Eternal Masters, Commander products, Modern Horizons 2, and Commander Masters.
