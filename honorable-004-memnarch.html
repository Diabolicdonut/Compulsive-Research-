The Machine That Could Make Everything His

Memnarch turned blue control into an act of annexation: first redefine the world as machinery, then claim the machinery permanently.

I · The sovereign

The machine that could make everything his

Memnarch does not destroy the opponent's best permanent. It does not copy it, borrow it for a turn, or merely prevent it from functioning. It changes the permanent's nature and then declares ownership.

The card is a seven-mana 4/5, an unremarkable body for its price even by the standards of 2004. Its importance begins beneath the power and toughness box. For {1}{U}{U}, any permanent becomes an artifact in addition to its other types. For {3}{U}, Memnarch gains control of an artifact. Neither effect expires. Given enough blue mana, creatures, lands, enchantments, planeswalkers, and opposing mana rocks all become parts of the same expanding machine.

That rules text captured the character more perfectly than a paragraph of flavor text could. Memnarch was the hidden ruler of Mirrodin, a custodian who had come to regard the plane and its people as components of his design. The card behaves the same way. It looks at an opponent's battlefield and sees no boundary between another player's property and its own future resources.

Memnarch was not the defining competitive card of Darksteel. That set contained Arcbound Ravager, Skullclamp, Aether Vial, Sword of Fire and Ice, and Darksteel Colossus. It instead became a slower kind of landmark: a tournament finisher once Urzatron could support it, a rules puzzle at the climax of a national championship, one of the earliest legendary creatures to translate naturally into Commander, and a permanent symbol of the player who would rather take the opposing board than merely defeat it.

II · The set

Darksteel and the wrong kind of terrifying

Darksteel entered an artifact block already operating near the limits of what artifacts could safely do. Mirrodin had supplied artifact lands, affinity, Chrome Mox, Mindslaver, and the engine pieces that would become the Affinity deck. Darksteel added modular creatures, indestructible permanents, and some of the most consequential cards of the era.

Memnarch looked enormous and frightening in a preview binder. Seven mana bought a legendary villain, two repeatable activated abilities, and the theoretical power to acquire anything on the battlefield. Yet the set's genuinely broken cards did not wait for seven mana. Ravager and Skullclamp converted cheap artifacts into immediate pressure and cards. Aether Vial ignored mana costs. Memnarch demanded that a deck survive, develop a huge mana base, cast a vulnerable creature, and then continue spending mana before the takeover began.

Darksteel's most dangerous cards made mana efficiency disappear. Memnarch asked what blue could do after mana efficiency no longer mattered.The difference between broken and inevitable

This distinction explains much of the card's history. Memnarch was rarely the reason a deck reached the late game. It was the reason the late game stopped being competitive once the deck had already reached it. It belonged not to the explosive artifact strategy but to the control strategy that could turn artifact-block mana engines against the rest of the format.

III · The story

The Warden of Argentum

Mirrodin was originally Argentum, an artificial plane created by Karn. Its warden became Memnarch: a sentient machine charged with watching the world in its creator's absence. The deeper storyline connects that warden to the Mirari, the powerful Dominaria artifact Karn reshaped into the core of his new guardian.

The assignment was protection. The result was possession. Contamination by Phyrexian oil, isolation, and resentment transformed Memnarch into a paranoid ruler. He renamed the plane Mirrodin, populated it by abducting beings from other planes, watched them from the Panopticon, and searched for a planeswalker spark he could seize for himself. The guardian's logic became totalitarian: if the plane was his responsibility, then everything on the plane could be treated as his material.

The card compresses that story into two activations. The first ability is classification. Memnarch imposes his preferred ontology on the target: whatever it was before, it is machinery now. The second ability is annexation. Once the permanent fits his system, control transfers permanently.

Story and mechanics align unusually closely. Memnarch's card does not merely feature a generic theft effect because blue can steal things. It reenacts the character's central delusion—that transforming a world gives him the right to own it.

This made the card feel like a final villain even to players who had never read the Mirrodin novels. The cost, scale, legendary frame, sprawling Carl Critchlow figure, and two-step control text all suggested a creature that was not entering combat so much as assuming authority over the game.

IV · The mechanism

Turn it to metal, then take it

Memnarch's abilities form one sentence written across two text boxes. An opposing artifact can be stolen directly for {3}{U}. Any other permanent requires the first ability to resolve before the second can target it.

1 · ChooseIdentify the permanent that matters most: a land, creature, enchantment, planeswalker, or existing artifact.
2 · ConvertPay {1}{U}{U}. The target becomes an artifact in addition to its other types.
3 · AcquirePay {3}{U}. Memnarch's second ability gains control of that artifact.
4 · KeepThe type change and control effect continue after the turn and after Memnarch leaves the battlefield.

The sequence matters. A player cannot announce the second ability targeting a nonartifact and expect the first ability to make the target legal later. The conversion must resolve first. Once it has, the permanent remains an artifact while it stays on the battlefield, so the control ability can be activated at a later time even if Memnarch is temporarily short of mana.

That delay creates interaction. An opponent can remove Memnarch after the conversion, sacrifice the newly metallic permanent, bounce it, or destroy it with artifact removal. Conversely, converting something can expose it to cards that would not otherwise affect it. Annul cannot retroactively counter it, but Shatter, Rebuild, and later effects such as Vandalblast may suddenly matter.

Memnarch therefore rewards sequencing rather than blind activation. Against open mana, the best target may be an existing artifact because it requires only one activation. Against a sacrifice outlet, converting a permanent can be a threat that forces the sacrifice even if the theft never occurs. Against a mana-screwed opponent, stealing a land may be more decisive than stealing the largest creature.

Carl Critchlow's original Darksteel artworkMemnarch is not depicted as a clean machine or a humanoid tyrant. He sprawls across the frame like a diseased industrial organism—part crab, part tower, part exposed nervous system—embodying the card's blur between metal, flesh, and madness.
V · The duration

The theft that does not expire

The parenthetical reminder on both abilities is essential: the effects last indefinitely. They are not tied to Memnarch remaining on the battlefield. Destroying Memnarch after a permanent has been stolen does not return that permanent. The control effect continues until another effect changes control or the object leaves the battlefield.

The same is true of the artifact type. A converted creature remains an artifact creature. A converted land remains an artifact land. If the permanent leaves and returns, it becomes a new object with no memory of Memnarch's effect, but merely removing Memnarch does not undo the transformation.

This makes one successful turn difficult to reverse cleanly. Killing the seven-mana creature answers future activations but not the resource already transferred. If Memnarch steals an opposing Vedalken Shackles, the controller keeps the Shackles. If he steals a land, the mana advantage remains. If he steals a commander in a multiplayer game and that commander stays on the battlefield, it remains under the new controller's command until another rule or effect moves it.

The card thus converts mana into durable card advantage rather than temporary tempo. A control deck that spends seven mana on Memnarch and four more to steal an artifact has not merely deployed a finisher. It has added the opponent's card to its own board while leaving a repeatable threat in play.

VI · The invoice

The price of absolute ownership

Memnarch's power is easy to describe and expensive to operate. Casting him costs seven. Stealing an artifact costs four more. Turning a nonartifact into an artifact and stealing it costs seven additional mana, including three blue mana across the two activations.

Cast Memnarch

{7} for a 4/5 that has not yet changed the battlefield.

Take an artifact

{3}{U} after Memnarch resolves. Total investment from an empty battlefield: eleven mana.

Take anything else

{1}{U}{U} to convert, then {3}{U} to gain control. Total investment from an empty battlefield: fourteen mana.

Repeat

Every surplus activation compounds the advantage. Once the first theft survives, the opponent is fighting both Memnarch and their former permanent.

Those numbers explain why ordinary midrange decks could not treat Memnarch as a conventional top-end creature. The card needed a mana system: the Urza lands, artifact acceleration, untapping engines, or the exaggerated economies of multiplayer Commander.

They also explain why Memnarch feels so oppressive after the threshold is crossed. The price is not balanced around a player making one enormous activation and stopping. It assumes that reaching this amount of mana is difficult. When a deck produces twenty or infinite mana, the constraint disappears and the effect becomes exactly as absolute as its wording suggests.

VII · The delay

Why 2004 passed it by

Memnarch arrived in February 2004, but the immediate Standard conversation belonged to Affinity, Skullclamp, Goblins, and anti-artifact decks. The format punished expensive cards in two opposite ways. Affinity could end the game before seven mana, while decks built to fight Affinity carried unusually high amounts of artifact destruction.

Tooth and Nail strategies had access to huge creatures without paying their printed mana costs, but Memnarch was not the cleanest creature pair or single bullet. Darksteel Colossus ended games quickly. Platinum Angel and Leonin Abunas formed a defensive lock. Triskelion with Mephidross Vampire cleared creatures. Memnarch needed continued blue mana after arriving, which green Tooth decks were poorly suited to provide.

It was not that the effect was weak. The format had not yet built the correct bridge between seven colorless mana and repeated blue activations. That bridge appeared when control decks adopted the Urza lands.

VIII · The engine

The deck that finally paid the bill

Urza's Mine, Urza's Power Plant, and Urza's Tower produce seven colorless mana when assembled. That number is printed in Memnarch's upper-right corner.

Blue Urzatron did not need to accelerate into a fast kill. It used Condescend, Mana Leak, Echoing Truth, and Thirst for Knowledge to survive while assembling the lands. Solemn Simulacrum fixed colored mana and bought time. Mindslaver created an opening. Memnarch then turned the mana engine into a permanent-control engine.

The relationship was unusually exact. Tron supplied the seven generic mana to cast Memnarch, while Islands, Talismans, Chrome Mox, and Solemn Simulacrum supplied blue for the activations. Once the three lands were active, the control deck could cast a threat and still approach the mana needed to protect it or activate it on the following turn.

Urzatron did not make Memnarch cheap. It made fourteen mana feel like a normal late-game turn.Blue control discovers industrial scale

Memnarch also gave the deck a way to exploit opponents who had stabilized against conventional finishers. A creature could be blocked. A Triskelion could expend its counters. Memnarch threatened the opponent's stabilizing cards themselves. Every permanent played to recover became another future target.

IX · The championship

U.S. Nationals 2005

Blue Urzatron broke through dramatically at the 2005 U.S. National Championship. Antonino De Rosa won the tournament with a version credited in contemporary coverage to Gerard Fabiano's tuning group. Jonathan Sonne finished third with another Memnarch Tron list, and Alex Lieberman also placed in the Top 8 with the archetype.

De Rosa's list played two Memnarchs, three Triskelions, three Mindslavers, and a dense card-selection package. The sideboard splashed black for Cranial Extraction, using a Swamp, Talismans, and Solemn Simulacrums to attack Tooth and Nail decks through Boseiju, Who Shelters All. The deck was not built around Memnarch alone; it was a layered control machine in which Memnarch was the most totalizing endgame.

The finals against Neil Reeves's mono-blue control deck gave the card its defining tournament stage. Mindslaver created windows in which Memnarch could resolve. Once it did, opposing Spire Golems and Vedalken Shackles were no longer merely threats to answer. They were assets waiting to change sides.

Memnarch was not incidental to the story. Contemporary coverage repeatedly returned to it as the board state became complicated. The national title was decided in a match full of counterspells, control-changing effects, and judges explaining what happened when one blue player tried to steal the artifact that was trying to steal the creature that could steal the artifact.

X · The stack

The finals became a rules lesson

The most remembered sequence involved Memnarch and Vedalken Shackles. Reeves could use Shackles to gain control of Memnarch. De Rosa could use Memnarch to gain control of the Shackles. Memnarch could also steal a Spire Golem while control-changing abilities waited on the stack.

The interaction illustrates a crucial rule: changing control of the source of an activated ability does not change who controls an ability already on the stack. Once a player activates Memnarch, that ability exists independently of Memnarch. Once Shackles has resolved, however, its continuous control effect depends on the Shackles remaining tapped and under the relevant conditions. Untapping or losing the Shackles can end that effect.

In the match, the players openly worked through the possibilities with the judges. One sequence left Reeves with Memnarch and De Rosa with a stolen Spire Golem before Mindslaver allowed De Rosa to undo the disaster. The scene was funny, tense, and perfectly suited to the card. Memnarch was not merely a large threat; it turned control of the game pieces into the subject of the game itself.

The card's championship moment was not an attack for four. It was a public demonstration that permanent theft, temporary control, tapped-state dependencies, and activated abilities can create a board where ownership must be resolved one object at a time.

This is a major reason Memnarch remained memorable after Blue Tron rotated. Many tournament finishers are remembered because they killed quickly. Memnarch is remembered because its presence made the table intellectually and politically unstable.

XI · The command zone

A colorless card with a blue command zone

Memnarch has a colorless mana cost and is an artifact creature, but the blue mana symbols in its rules text give it a blue color identity in Commander. A Memnarch deck can therefore use blue and colorless cards, Islands, and blue-producing lands, but no cards with white, black, red, or green color identity.

That technical distinction became part of the card's identity. Memnarch looks like the commander of a colorless artifact deck, yet plays as an extreme expression of blue: countermagic, card draw, artifact tutoring, control-changing effects, and enormous mana engines.

Commander also solves the availability problem. A seven-mana legendary creature can be cast from the command zone whenever the deck is ready. The entire ninety-nine can be designed to accelerate it, protect it, reduce its activated costs, untap mana artifacts, or produce infinite mana. The card no longer has to be drawn as one of two copies near the top of a control deck. It is the declared endpoint of the deck.

This made Memnarch one of the natural early artifact commanders. Long before the format received a constant supply of legends designed specifically for Commander, Memnarch already offered a complete build-around instruction: make mana, turn the board into artifacts, and acquire it.

XII · The table

The commander that announces the threat

Memnarch creates a political problem before it is cast. Players know what seven open mana and a command-zone Memnarch imply. Mana rocks are vulnerable. Commanders are vulnerable. Lands can become vulnerable. The deck does not need a hidden combo to explain why it must be watched.

This transparency is both strength and weakness. Memnarch can dominate a game in which opponents spend resources on one another and allow the artifact player to develop. It can also make its controller the archenemy from turn zero. Theft is more emotionally provocative than removal because the victim must continue looking at their card on the other side of the table.

Good Memnarch play therefore involves threat selection. Stealing the strongest permanent immediately may be correct in a high-power game. In a social game, taking a modest mana rock or an essential combo piece can control the board without convincing every opponent that nothing they cast will remain theirs. The commander supplies no diplomacy on its own; its controller must create it.

That tension is part of the appeal. Memnarch decks are rarely subtle in concept, but they can be subtle in timing. The most dangerous activation is often not the first one. It is the moment the table realizes the Memnarch player can perform the sequence repeatedly and still hold up a counterspell.

XIII · The factory

Mana, untapping, and the industrial engine

Memnarch scales more dramatically with mana than almost any conventional creature. Extra mana is not merely used to cast additional cards; it becomes direct access to the opposing battlefield.

Training Grounds reduces the generic portion of the abilities, turning the conversion into {U}{U} and the theft into {1}{U}. Unwinding Clock untaps artifact mana during every opponent's untap step. Rings of Brighthearth can copy activations. Clock of Omens, Basalt Monolith, Power Artifact, Grand Architect, and Pili-Pala represent the kind of untapping and infinite-mana infrastructure that turns the commander from expensive control card into deterministic endgame.

Protection is equally important. Lightning Greaves can protect Memnarch but shroud prevents its controller from targeting it with their own beneficial effects; Swiftfoot Boots is slower but less restrictive. Countermagic often does more than equipment because it can defend both Memnarch and the stolen board.

The deck's characteristic turn is industrial rather than explosive. It assembles mana rocks and cost reducers, deploys Memnarch when it can retain priority or protect him, and then converts every untap step into an acquisition budget.

XIV · The world machine

When everything is already an artifact

Mycosynth Lattice is Memnarch's most literal companion. If all permanents are artifacts in addition to their other types, the first ability becomes unnecessary. Every permanent can be targeted by the {3}{U} control ability immediately.

The interaction is powerful, obvious, and dangerous. Lattice also makes the entire battlefield vulnerable to artifact hate. A single mass-artifact destruction spell can become land destruction. Opponents know that a resolved Lattice beside Memnarch means every land and commander is four mana away from changing sides.

The flavor is almost too exact. Mirrodin's mycosynth blurred metal and flesh, the same boundary Memnarch's abilities manipulate. The card pair creates a mechanical version of the plane under his rule: everything has already been incorporated into the machine, and ownership is the only remaining question.

The remnant

Urtet remembers the machine

Urtet, Remnant of Memnarch returned Memnarch's name to a modern card and tied his legacy to the Myr. The descendant does not steal the board, but its title confirms how deeply the vanished tyrant remains embedded in Mirrodin's mechanical history.

Memnarch also pairs naturally with Liquimetal Coating and Liquimetal Torque. These cards duplicate the first ability for one target while providing utility or mana. They show how Memnarch's once-bizarre type-changing effect became a reusable design tool: turning a permanent into an artifact can enable theft, removal, animation, sacrifice, or synergy.

XV · The inheritance

The machines that followed

Memnarch sits at the intersection of several design families rather than founding only one. Blue had stolen permanents before through Control Magic and Treachery. Artifacts had changed types before. Large mana sinks had existed since Alpha. Memnarch joined all three into a repeatable engine.

Later cards separated the pieces. Liquimetal effects made permanents into artifacts. Master Thief, Thada Adel, Acquisitor, and Emry, Lurker of the Loch explored blue's relationship with opposing or reusable artifacts. Agent of Treachery carried unconditional permanent theft onto a creature, though as a one-time trigger rather than a mana engine.

Artifact commanders also became far more efficient. Arcum Dagsson tutors artifacts directly. Urza, Lord High Artificer turns artifacts into mana and cards. Mishra, Eminent One copies them. Memnarch remains distinct because its central resource is not merely its own artifact collection. It is everybody else's.

That is why the card has not been functionally replaced. Newer commanders are cheaper, faster, or more synergistic, but few state their ambition as plainly. Memnarch does not ask the deck to build an army. It asks the deck to finance a hostile acquisition.

XVI · The image

Carl Critchlow and the body of a tyrant

Carl Critchlow's original image refuses a simple silhouette. Memnarch has long segmented limbs, a hunched central body, cables or tendons, clawed appendages, and a luminous cranial structure. The figure resembles a machine designed by something that remembers anatomy but no longer respects it.

The low angle makes the viewer feel small, but the creature's posture is not heroic. It is invasive. Limbs spread across the architecture as though Memnarch and the Panopticon are becoming the same organism. The blue light in the head suggests intellect, surveillance, and magical control rather than warmth.

The flavor text—“In the blur between metal and flesh, Memnarch found madness”—does not explain the rules. It explains the image. The card's two activated abilities then show what that madness does: erase categories and ownership.

Later printings treated Memnarch as lore rather than merely a Darksteel rare. Archenemy placed him in a preconstructed machine deck. From the Vault: Lore selected him as one of the story-significant cards of Magic's history. A Secret Lair reinterpretation demonstrated that the character had become visually recognizable enough to survive a radical new presentation.

XVII · The recollection

What players remember

“I did not remove your best permanent. I improved my board with it.”
The emotional logic of a Memnarch activation

Players who met Memnarch in 2004 often remember the apparent infinity of the text box. A new player could read the card and immediately imagine taking every permanent in play. Whether the game ever lasted long enough was secondary. The possibility was the fantasy.

Tournament players remember Blue Tron and the 2005 Nationals finals: Memnarch beside Mindslaver, the Urza lands producing absurd mana, and a Vedalken Shackles interaction complicated enough to stop the match for explanation.

Commander players remember the social reaction. Memnarch is one of the commanders whose name can change mulligan decisions before a card is drawn. It creates stories of stolen commanders, stolen lands, desperate removal spells, and the moment an infinite-mana engine turns the battlefield into inventory.

Collectors remember a strange visual object from Darksteel: a legendary artifact creature whose body seemed to contain the whole block's discomfort with the boundary between organism and machine. Lore readers remember the Mirari's transformation, Karn's absence, and the guardian whose attempt to control Mirrodin helped prepare the world for catastrophe.

XVIII · The appendix

Why it belongs here

Memnarch is not among the archive's numbered one thousand, but it embodies too many parts of Magic's history to disappear outside the list.

It was a named antagonist translated unusually well into rules text. It became a genuine championship finisher when Blue Urzatron found the mana to support it. It produced one of the most memorable high-level control interactions of its era. It later found a permanent home in Commander, where its legendary status, artifact identity, blue activations, and political threat all became more important than they had been in sixty-card formats.

Most importantly, Memnarch represents a specific kind of Magic fantasy. Some cards promise speed. Some promise efficiency. Memnarch promises that, given time and mana, the distinction between your battlefield and the opponent's battlefield will cease to exist.

That promise was rarely fair, rarely subtle, and never forgotten.

XX · The archive

Printings and sources

Memnarch first appeared as rare number 129 in Darksteel, illustrated by Carl Critchlow. The card returned in Archenemy, appeared in From the Vault: Lore, and later received a Secret Lair treatment. Oracle text preserves the original two-stage mechanism while explicitly reminding players that both effects last indefinitely.

The essential identity

Memnarch turns excess blue mana into a permanent claim on whatever the opponent believed was theirs.
